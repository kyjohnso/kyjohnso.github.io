🌴 Tropical Rainforests
Ecosystems · world environments · climate factors · importance · deforestation

🌿 Ecosystems & world environments

  • An ecosystem is a community of living things that interact with each other and with the non-living environment (climate, soil, water).
  • A biome is a large-scale global ecosystem — e.g. tropical rainforest, hot desert, savanna grassland, tundra, polar.
  • Biotic = the living parts (plants, animals). Abiotic = the non-living parts (sunlight, temperature, water, soil).
  • Energy flows through a food chain / food web: producers (plants) → consumers (animals) → decomposers (break dead matter down and recycle nutrients).

🌡️ Factors affecting climate

  • Latitude — distance from the Equator. Near the Equator = hot; near the poles = cold.
  • Altitude — height above sea level. The higher you go, the colder it gets.
  • Distance from the sea — the sea heats/cools slowly, so it moderates coastal temperatures.
  • Ocean currents and prevailing winds carry warmth or cold to different places.

📍 Where & what are tropical rainforests?

  • Found around the Equator, between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn — the Amazon (S. America), the Congo (Africa), and South-East Asia.
  • Climate is hot (~27°C) and wet (~2000mm rain) all year — there are no real seasons.
  • Four layers: emergent (tallest trees, 45m+) → canopy (dense roof ~30m, most wildlife) → under-canopy → dark forest floor.
  • Plant adaptations: drip-tip leaves (shed heavy rain), buttress roots (support tall trees in thin soil), lianas and epiphytes (climb/grow up to reach light).

💚 Why rainforests matter

  • Incredible biodiversity — the greatest variety of species on Earth.
  • The "lungs of the planet" — they release oxygen and absorb CO₂ (a giant carbon sink), helping control climate.
  • Source of medicines, foods and materials, and home to indigenous peoples.
  • Help regulate the water cycle.

🪓 Deforestation

  • Causes: logging for timber, clearing for farming (cattle ranching, palm oil, soya), mining, roads, settlement and dams.
  • Effects: lost biodiversity, more CO₂ → climate change, soil erosion & flooding, and indigenous people losing their homes.
  • Solutions: selective logging, replanting, ecotourism, agroforestry and protected reserves (sustainable management).

🏙️ Urbanisation
Manchester's story · megacities · push & pull · slums & solutions

🔑 Key words

  • Urbanisation = the increase in the proportion of people living in towns and cities.
  • Urban = town/city; rural = countryside.
  • Megacity = a city with over 10 million people (e.g. Tokyo, Delhi, São Paulo, Lagos).

🏭 Manchester's story

  • Industrialisation (1800s): Manchester boomed on the cotton/textile industry — nicknamed "Cottonopolis" — pulling in workers; canals & railways grew with it.
  • Decline (1900s): mills and factories closed as industry moved abroad — unemployment, dereliction and people leaving the inner city.
  • Regeneration (recent): run-down areas redeveloped — Salford Quays / MediaCityUK, new housing, sport and services (rebuilding after the 1996 city-centre bomb).

➡️ Push & pull factors

  • Push factors drive people away from the countryside: poverty, few jobs, drought / crop failure, poor services, natural disasters.
  • Pull factors attract people to cities: better-paid jobs, education, healthcare, services and entertainment.
  • The fastest-growing cities today are in lower-income countries (LICs/NEEs) across Asia, Africa and South America.

🏚️ Living in slums & tackling the problem

  • A slum / squatter settlement / shanty town is a poor, overcrowded area, often built illegally — e.g. Dharavi in Mumbai.
  • Problems: overcrowding, poor sanitation and disease, no clean water or electricity, and informal jobs (no contract — street selling, recycling).
  • Solutions: self-help / site-and-service schemes (residents given materials & loans to improve homes), upgrading water, sanitation and power, and help from NGOs/charities.

🌊 Coasts
Waves · erosion, transport & deposition · landforms · management

🌬️ What causes waves?

  • Waves are made by the wind blowing across the sea surface (friction).
  • Fetch = the distance of open water the wind blows over. Bigger fetch = bigger waves.
  • Constructive waves build beaches up (strong swash, weak backwash). Destructive waves erode (weak swash, strong backwash).

⛏️ The four processes of erosion

  • Hydraulic action — the force of water compresses air in cracks and shatters the rock.
  • Abrasion — waves fling rocks & sand at the cliff, wearing it away like sandpaper.
  • Attrition — rocks knock together, becoming smaller, rounder and smoother.
  • Solution — acids in seawater chemically dissolve rock such as limestone.

↗️ Transportation — longshore drift

  • The prevailing wind pushes waves up the beach at an angle (swash); backwash drags material straight back down (gravity).
  • This makes sediment zig-zag along the coast — that's longshore drift.
  • Material is also moved by traction, saltation, suspension and solution.

✏️ Sketch this! Cave → Arch → Stack → Stump

Waves attack a crack → cave → erodes through the headland → arch → roof collapses → stack → stack erodes down to a stump. Practise drawing & labelling this for the exam!

🏖️ Landforms & coastal management

  • Erosion landforms: headlands & bays (hard vs soft rock), cliffs & wave-cut platforms, and cave → arch → stack → stump.
  • Deposition landforms: beaches, and spits (a ridge of sand built by longshore drift, joined to land at one end).
  • Hard engineering: sea walls, groynes (trap sediment), rock armour (rip-rap), gabions.
  • Soft engineering: beach nourishment, managed retreat, dune regeneration.

💡 Revision Tips
Straight from your revision guide

How to revise

  • Sketch & label diagrams to explain what happens (essential for Coasts!).
  • Look, cover, write, check important facts.
  • Flash cards — key term on one side, definition or answer on the other.
  • Mind maps — pick a key word and branch out with details.
  • Test yourself — get someone to quiz you, then focus on what you didn't know.

Remember

  • You'll do recall questions and show skills: identifying places on a map, describing what you see, and explaining your ideas.
  • Learn the geographical terms from each topic — they win marks.
